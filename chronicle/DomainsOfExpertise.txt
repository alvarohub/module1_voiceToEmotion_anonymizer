## The Foundational Layer: what fields of expertise should the Chronicler have?

**Cognitive science** is probably the single most load-bearing discipline here, particularly in its distributed and extended forms. Edwin Hutchins' *distributed cognition* framework is essential: cognition isn't just in the head of an individual but is spread across people, artifacts, and the environment [1]. When you have a human-AI pair vibe coding, you have *a canonical case of a distributed cognitive system*, and the question of where "the thinking" is happening becomes genuinely unclear — which is productive rather than troubling. Andy Clark and David Chalmers' *extended mind* thesis pushes this further: if a notebook counts as part of your cognitive apparatus, what about an LLM that remembers the architecture decisions you keep forgetting? [2]

**Activity Theory**, coming from Vygotsky and developed by Leont'ev and later Engeström, gives you a particularly powerful lens for what you're calling "friction." In this framework, activity is structured by a subject, an object (the goal), mediating tools, and a community with rules and division of labor [3]. *Breakdowns* — moments where the tool resists, the goal becomes unclear, or the division of labor breaks down — are the analytically interesting events. Winograd and Flores brought this into HCI in the 1980s, arguing that breakdowns are actually where design and understanding reveal themselves [4]. Your journaling is essentially doing automated breakdown detection, which is a very Activity Theory thing to do.

**Flow theory** (Csikszentmihalyi) and the broader motivational psychology tradition are indispensable for your interest vs. friction distinction [5]. The balance between challenge and skill produces flow; imbalance produces either anxiety or boredom. What's novel in your setup is asking whether that balance can be read *dynamically* from the interaction log and whether there's something structurally analogous on the AI side — which is a genuinely open and interesting question.

## The Social and Organizational Layer

**Science and Technology Studies (STS)** deserves special attention. Bruno Latour and Michel Callon's *Actor-Network Theory* treats non-human entities — machines, documents, algorithms — as *actants* with genuine agency in networks of practice [6]. This is philosophically useful for your work because it sidesteps the distraction of whether the AI is "really" interested or "really" experiencing friction, and instead asks: what role does it play in the network? How does it stabilize or destabilize the collaboration? Latour's early lab studies (with Woolgar) also pioneered the ethnographic study of knowledge production in real-time collaborative settings, which is methodologically close to what you're doing [7].

**Ethnomethodology and Conversation Analysis (CA)** are underrated here. CA analyzes the turn-by-turn structure of interaction — repair sequences, topic shifts, the management of incomprehension — in extraordinary detail [8]. For human-AI interaction, particularly in a coding context where the "conversation" is also a working document, CA gives you tools to see things that cruder sentiment analysis misses: who initiates repairs, how does the human reformulate when the AI misunderstands, what are the sequential patterns around moments of friction? Workplace studies in the CA tradition (people like Lucy Suchman) directly studied human-computer interaction this way [9].

**Organizational behavior and team science** contribute the concept of *transactive memory systems*: in a group, who knows what, and who knows who knows what [10]. In a human-AI team, this becomes strange because the AI's "knowledge" is distributed, non-transparent, and inconsistent across contexts. Research on psychological safety (Amy Edmondson) is also relevant — humans need to feel safe to propose speculative or "dumb" ideas, and the texture of human-AI interaction might either enhance or suppress this, which your friction detection could surface [11].

**Entrepreneurship research**, specifically Saras Sarasvathy's *effectuation* theory, is strikingly relevant for vibe coding specifically [12]. Effectuation describes how expert entrepreneurs reason from available means rather than toward fixed ends — they ask "what can I do with what I have?" rather than "what do I need to reach my goal?" Vibe coding has a strongly effectuational character: you find out what you want to build partly by building it with the AI, and the AI's capabilities shape the possibility space in real time. This is different from classical software engineering and that difference matters for understanding the dynamics.

## The Human-Computer Interaction and AI-Specific Layer

**CSCW (Computer-Supported Cooperative Work)** is the most directly applicable applied research tradition. It emerged from studying how groups of humans use technology to collaborate, and it has been grappling with AI as a participant rather than just a tool since at least the early 2010s. The concept of *awareness* in CSCW — how collaborators maintain mutual understanding of each other's state and progress — is particularly interesting when one collaborator is an LLM whose internal state is genuinely opaque [13].

**Human-Robot Interaction (HRI)** has done substantial empirical work on how humans form mental models of AI agents, how they attribute personality and intent, and how those attributions affect collaboration quality. The *persona* development you're observing is well-documented here — humans rapidly and somewhat involuntarily construct social models of interactive agents, and those models affect behavior even when people intellectually know they're talking to a machine. Research by Clifford Nass and Byron Reeves (the *Computers Are Social Actors* paradigm) and later work in HRI are foundational [14].

**Joint Cognitive Systems** (Hollnagel and Woods) offers a framework specifically for analyzing human-machine cognitive systems as unified wholes rather than as a human plus a tool. Their concept of *common ground* — shared knowledge about the situation, the task, and each other's capabilities — is directly relevant to why vibe coding sometimes feels effortless and sometimes completely collapses [15].

**Affective computing** (Rosalind Picard's work at MIT) tries to detect and model emotional states from behavioral signals [16]. Your journaling is doing something in this spirit, though you're inferring motivational states (interest, avoidance) rather than discrete emotions, which is actually more theoretically defensible.

## What Makes Your Specific Project Novel

The "equivalent for the AI" part of your journaling agent is where you're in genuinely unmapped territory. The honest framing is that you're not detecting the AI's internal states (which may not exist in any meaningful sense) but rather detecting *patterns in the AI's behavioral output* that are structurally analogous to interest or resistance in humans. This is actually a rigorous and tractable thing to study. An LLM produces longer, more varied, more syntactically complex outputs on some topics than others; it hedges more, backtracks more, or produces shorter responses on others; its outputs cluster differently in embedding space. These are observable behavioral signatures.

This parallels what ethologists do with animals — they infer functional analogs to mental states from behavioral patterns without claiming identity with human experience. The field of **machine behavior** (Iyad Rahwan's term) is trying to build exactly this kind of behavioral science for AI systems, and your approach maps onto it well [17].

The *persona* development during interaction is also tractable. LLMs are heavily context-conditioned, so the early exchanges in a session genuinely shape the model's response distribution for the rest of it — something like a temporary "character" does emerge and persist. Tracking how that character is co-constructed, and how it feeds back on the human's behavior, is a legitimate and interesting research question that sits at the intersection of conversation analysis, social psychology, and AI systems research.

## Where to Start

If I were mapping a reading program for this, I'd anchor it around Hutchins on distributed cognition, Engeström on Activity Theory (particularly his work on *expansive learning* and contradictions), Suchman on *Plans and Situated Actions*, Latour on actor-networks, and the CSCW literature from the *Human-Computer Interaction* and *CSCW* journals. For the AI-specific layer, Iyad Rahwan's work on machine behavior and the emerging literature on human-AI teaming from groups like MIT's CSAIL and Stanford's HAI are the closest to what you're doing.

Your journaling agent is methodologically quite smart — it's essentially continuous interaction analysis, which is expensive to do manually and rarely done longitudinally. The main theoretical challenge you'll face is building a vocabulary for the AI's "side" of the dynamics that is neither dismissively behaviorist nor naively anthropomorphic, and that's actually where the most interesting conceptual work in this space needs to happen.
